MangalKit

Curated puja hampers where devotion meets craft. Handmade paper packaging, wax seals, Sanskrit shlokas, saffron thread. Not a product — a feeling.

"Beta, yeh wahi lag raha hai
jo mandir se milta tha."

— My mother, holding the first MangalKit prototype
01 · Problem

Devotion had lost its packaging.

Puja products in India are either too commercial (garish plastic, generic branding, zero soul) or too archaic (dusty shops, no curation, nothing that feels like a gift). There's a massive gap in the middle: devotees who want to send their parents, grandparents, or loved ones something that feels sacred — not transactional.

People still believe in puja. They still want to bless. They just can't find something that feels worthy of the intention behind it. The faith is there. The product isn't.

02 · Solution

A box of blessings — curated, crafted, intentional.

MangalKit is not a puja product company. MangalKit is a feeling company. Every element of the kit is chosen to evoke the same reverence you feel at a temple — and then some.

The packaging: Handmade paper, aged in texture, wrapped by hand. A wax seal pressed with intention. An old-style paper shloka from Sanskrit scripture folded inside.

The name: Mangal (मंगल) — auspiciousness, blessing, well-being. Kit — a curated set of everything you need. Together: a box of blessings.

The identity: A diya in the logo for the flame of devotion. A lotus for purity and new beginnings. Saffron for courage and sacrifice. Every visual decision is a deliberate act of respect for tradition.

03 · The Team

Built with people who believe in what we're building.

MangalKit is being built with Ashutosh Kumar Singh, Kaustubh Shukla, Shivam Kumar Singh, and Mandeep Sharma. We are building in public — sharing every milestone, every failure, every breakthrough across 30 days.

This is Day 6 of 30. The prototype is wrapped. The seal is pressed. The mission is clear.

04 · The Moment That Defined It

Some moments don't need photos. They stay in the heart.

When we wrapped the first prototype and my mother held it — she didn't look at the price tag or the branding deck. She held it like it was already blessed. She said it felt like what you receive from a temple.

That was the validation we needed. Not a spreadsheet. Not a market report. A mother from Bihar, holding a box her son built, feeling something real.

MangalKit = Blessings. Delivered.
